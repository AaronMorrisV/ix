What is the F1 in Schools challenge..?

F1 in Schools is the world's largest STEM competition in which 20 million students from 44 countries compete in teams of 3-6 as they are tasked with designing and manufacturing a scaled model F1 car as well as creating and resourcing many other entities including portfolios, pit display, sponsorship and team identity. FAQs

How can I compete?

Assemble a team of 3-6 people, allocate job roles, read through this, then register here

How old do I have to be?

All members must be between the ages of 9 and 19

Why should I compete?

The F1 in Schools challenge provides life changing opportunities, as well as providing you the perfect platform on which to express your creativity and intellect all the while helping you to learn useful life skills regardless of future occupation

What skills do I need?

None. You don't need any specific skills to begin with as you will learn them in due course, however the main roles of an F1iS team are: Team Manager, Resources Manager, Manufacturing Engineer, Design Engineer and Graphic Designer, so some development in the appropriate skills for each role would be useful

Main Tasks

The following range of tasks are designed to utilise each individual's skills of technology, social engineering and creativity.

  • Design and manufacture a:
    • Model F1 car
    • Pit Display
  • Create two portfolios
  • Successfully operate as a team
  • Create a team identity
  • Fundraise
  • Raise sponsorship
  • Market

The Car

Discover

Chassis

The chassis of our body is quite flat, with a small frontal surface area, and therefore a lower drag coefficient. The front wing style structure before the front wheels, acts as a front wing would. The body sweeps around the rear wheels in a teardrop shape, with an inverted diffuser to generate lift.

Front

The nose cone is designed to push air over as well as away from the main body of the car, which is useful in reducing surface friction. After a lot of testing we found that a swept and dihedral wing was most efficient in producing the least drag and ensuring stability.

Rear

Sitting flush into the car body, the rear wing is the same shape as the front wing, being swept and dihedral. We have done this as the wing doesn’t affect the flow over the rest of the car body, so it only needs to have the lowest drag coefficient possible. The endplates ensure that lift produced is maximal.
